Three directions for paytonjunkin.com

Three home page concepts built against the Vibe & Reference Pack and the JC2026 Style Guide. All three follow the same brand law — ivory, navy, light blue, photography-led, no popups, no urgency, mobile-first. They differ in personality, density, and where the search bar lives.

The question to answer when comparing them isn't "which is prettier" — all three can be made beautiful. The question is which one is yours: which feels like the way you'd talk about Birmingham real estate to someone you respect, on a Tuesday, over coffee.

If a section in any of these doesn't feel right, that's good information. Open them on your phone too — mobile is where 60-70% of these will get viewed.

Reading note · search sections marked tentative Each mockup includes a property search bar. The exact behavior of that search depends on what your Lofty plan supports for external integration. We'll lock that in once we know your plan capabilities — so for now, treat the search section in any version as a placeholder for one of three approaches: an embedded Lofty widget, a custom UI built on Lofty's API, or a handoff to your existing Lofty search subdomain.

One coordination piece: we'll need John to grant us broker-level access to the Lofty account to verify the plan's external-integration capabilities, generate any API keys, configure the IDX widget styling, and confirm lead routing back to your CRM. Your end-user seat doesn't expose those settings — that's standard for any Lofty configuration on a brokerage account, not a workaround. Worth a quick heads-up to John so we don't lose time on Payton-Daniel ping-pong.

Direction three

Concierge

Split hero — photo on the left, welcome panel with search on the right. Below: three balanced columns of recent listings, Houses I Love, and journal entries.

  • Most familiar pattern — lowest cognitive load

Open mockup →
Why we lean toward Concierge (direction three) Three reasons. One, it's the most balanced — it serves buyers searching, sellers reading testimonials, and browsers reading the journal, without forcing any one of them through the others' content. Two, the search panel sits inside the hero alongside a personal welcome from you, so the search experience can be a Lofty widget without breaking the editorial promise the rest of the page is making. Three, it's the lowest-risk pattern — the one Payton can grow into over the next six months without re-architecting. That said, we're presenting all three because the right answer is the one you would come home to. If Editorial-First (direction two) feels more like you, that's a stronger answer than the safer one.
